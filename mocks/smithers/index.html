The operators described the alarm system as "something you learn to hear past" — a phrase that recurred, unprompted, in four of the six interviews we conducted on site this spring.

Nobody had disabled anything. The tolerance was social, not technical: a shared understanding, never written down, of which sounds meant now and which meant eventually. That distinction lived entirely in the crew's collective memory, and it walked out the door with every retirement.

When we asked what would happen if the whole night shift turned over at once, the answer came back with unusual speed. "Then you'd have a room full of people who believe every alarm." The line was delivered as a joke. It was not received as one.
